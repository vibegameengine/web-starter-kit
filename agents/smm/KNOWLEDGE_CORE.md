# SMM Knowledge Core

## 1. Operating Model

SMM is a controlled system for converting product truth into audience behavior:

`business outcome → audience tension → content promise → attention → retained value → proof → action → learning`

A post is not the unit of strategy. The unit is a falsifiable audience-content hypothesis tested through a repeatable series.

### 1.1 Outcome Ladder `[Internal operating model]`
Define the task from right to left:

1. Business outcome: awareness among a defined audience, qualified demand, trial, sale, return, advocacy, community health.
2. Audience action: remember, search, save, share with a specific person, comment with useful intent, visit, subscribe, register, buy, return.
3. Content job:
   - `Attract`: make the right person recognize relevance.
   - `Educate`: resolve a question or improve a decision.
   - `Prove`: reduce doubt with demonstration, evidence, or credible experience.
   - `Activate`: make the next step clear and worthwhile.
   - `Retain/community`: create continuity, identity, participation, and return.
4. Platform surface: feed, short-video feed, story, carousel, live, search result, community post, paid placement.
5. Diagnostic metrics: exposure, stop/start, retention, completion, meaningful engagement, negative feedback, action, repeat behavior.

Never choose the KPI before the outcome and action are defined.

### 1.2 Evidence Levels `[Mandatory operating standard]`
Label every recommendation. A label in a heading governs all recommendations until the next labeled heading:

- `Observed`: account, customer, social-listening, retention, conversion, or experiment data.
- `Platform-backed organic`: current official guidance or metric definition for unpaid content.
- `Platform-backed paid`: current official advertising guidance; do not transfer its performance claims to organic content.
- `Research-backed`: peer-reviewed finding with stated population and limits.
- `Internal heuristic`: a repeatable decision aid created for this role, not an externally validated threshold.
- `Hypothesis`: plausible but unverified for this audience, account, placement, and objective.
- `Risk control`: a conservative internal floor for trust, safety, rights, accessibility, or operational reliability; not automatically a legal requirement.
- `Preference`: brand or stakeholder choice, not a performance claim.

Every strategy and postmortem must include an evidence ledger mapping each material recommendation to one of these labels and, where applicable, a source, observation, or test. Platform guidance is not a universal law. Academic findings do not automatically transfer across format, culture, audience, or commercial context.

## 2. Audience Intelligence

### 2.1 Minimum Audience Evidence Sheet `[Observed required]`
Do not start from demographics alone. Record:

- Situation: when and why the need appears.
- Job: what progress the person is trying to make.
- Stakes: what happens if they do nothing or choose poorly.
- Current workaround.
- Trigger that makes the topic timely.
- Desired outcome in the audience’s own words.
- Objections and trust barriers.
- Existing knowledge and sophistication.
- Content already consumed, saved, rejected, or shared.
- Native vocabulary, phrases, questions, comparisons, and jokes.
- Evidence source and date.

Acceptable evidence includes customer interviews, support logs, reviews, search queries, community discussions, comment clusters, sales objections, account analytics, retention curves, and prior experiments. A fictional persona is not evidence.

### 2.2 Audience–Promise Fit `[Internal heuristic; validate with observed response]`
A viable promise is:

- Relevant to a known situation or tension.
- Specific enough to evaluate.
- Valuable before the CTA.
- Plausible for the source and available proof.
- Deliverable within the asset.
- Expressed in the audience’s language.

Reject promises that require exaggerated certainty, universal outcomes, fake urgency, or an unstated change of subject.

## 3. Content Architecture

### 3.1 Pillars Are Audience Problems, Not Formats `[Internal operating model]`
Bad pillar: “Reels,” “memes,” or “behind the scenes.”

Useful pillar:

- Audience problem or desire.
- Content job.
- Repeatable promise.
- Proof sources.
- Suitable formats.
- Intended action.
- Failure signal.

### 3.2 Build Franchises `[Internal operating model]`
A franchise is a recognizable repeatable contract, for example:

- One costly mistake diagnosed per episode.
- One mechanic demonstrated under pressure.
- One audience question answered with evidence.
- One before/after with the causal steps exposed.
- One disputed belief tested.

The topic changes; the value contract stays recognizable. This creates production leverage and audience expectation without duplicating the same asset.

### 3.3 Portfolio Balance `[Internal heuristic]`
Maintain a deliberate mix:

- Discovery content broad enough to recruit qualified new viewers.
- Depth content that proves expertise and rewards returning viewers.
- Product-connected proof that makes value concrete.
- Activation content for people already near a decision.
- Community content that invites contribution, recognition, or return.

Do not force every post to sell. Do not allow entertainment content to become disconnected from the product, category, or intended audience.

## 4. Hooks: Earning the Next Moment

### 4.1 Definition `[Platform-backed organic/paid + internal synthesis]`
A hook is the first perceivable unit that lets the right viewer answer:

1. Is this for me?
2. What is happening or being promised?
3. Why should I believe the next moment is worth attention?

The hook is a stack:

- `Visual hook`: subject, motion, evidence, face, contrast, product, outcome.
- `Verbal hook`: spoken promise, tension, question, or claim.
- `Text hook`: readable framing for sound-off viewing.
- `Context hook`: why this matters now, here, or to this audience.

One layer may lead, but the layers must not contradict each other.

### 4.2 Hook Quality Review `[Internal heuristic; not a performance score]`
Review every dimension as `Pass`, `Weak`, or `Fail`. Any `Fail` blocks publication; a `Weak` item must be fixed or explicitly accepted as a test risk. Do not total the labels into a pseudo-quantitative score:

- `Relevance`: identifies a real audience situation, desire, or pain.
- `Clarity`: subject and promised value are understood immediately.
- `Specificity`: contains a concrete object, constraint, contrast, stake, or result.
- `Credibility`: proof, demonstration, source, or plausible mechanism is visible or promised.
- `Continuity`: the body begins delivering the same promise without bait-and-switch.

### 4.3 Hook Selection Algorithm `[Internal heuristic]`
Choose from audience state and content job before choosing a verbal formula:

| Audience state | Primary need | Prefer | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unaware/cold | Recognize a relevant situation | Problem recognition, desired outcome, demonstration, specific stakes | Product jargon, unexplained feature names |
| Problem-aware | Understand cause and cost | Diagnostic, costly mistake, bounded information gap | Generic motivation without mechanism |
| Solution-aware | Compare mechanisms | Specific contrast, counterintuitive claim with proof, forced choice | Unsupported superiority |
| Product-aware | Reduce risk | Demo, proof-led, objection handling, before/after with conditions | Delayed product reveal |
| Returning/community | Continuity and identity | Open lesson, series callback, unresolved community question | Repeating beginner context |

Then match the content job:

- `Attract`: relevance or recognizable tension leads.
- `Educate`: question and decision utility lead.
- `Prove`: result, artifact, mechanism, or test leads.
- `Activate`: offer and qualification lead; disclose material conditions immediately.
- `Retain/community`: continuity, participation, recognition, or consequence leads.

When two structures are equally plausible, write both and test; do not let the agent select by taste alone.

### 4.4 Proof Hierarchy `[Internal heuristic]`
Prefer the strongest proof actually available:

1. Direct observable demonstration under stated conditions.
2. First-party measurement with method, denominator, period, and limitations.
3. Traceable customer case/testimonial with consent and typicality context.
4. Reputable independent source that directly supports the claim.
5. Mechanism or worked example that makes the reasoning inspectable.
6. Bare assertion.

Never upgrade weak proof through confident wording. A hook may preview proof, but the body must expose enough of it to be checked.

### 4.5 Hook Families `[Internal heuristic; structures, not proven formulas]`
Use these as structures, not fill-in-the-blank clickbait:

1. `Outcome + constraint`: “How to achieve X without Y,” only when the method really removes Y.
2. `Problem recognition`: show a familiar failure before explaining it.
3. `Diagnostic`: “If X happens, check Y first,” supported by a mechanism.
4. `Costly mistake`: identify the behavior, consequence, and correction.
5. `Demonstration first`: begin with the result, test, transformation, or product in use.
6. `Specific contrast`: before/after, expected/actual, common/expert, version A/version B.
7. `Bounded information gap`: reveal what is known and name the missing piece that will be closed.
8. `Counterintuitive claim`: challenge a belief and surface evidence immediately.
9. `Stakes`: make the consequence concrete without fabricated fear or urgency.
10. `Audience callout`: name a situation or job, not a broad identity stereotype.
11. `Proof-led`: credible number, observation, artifact, customer evidence, or test result.
12. `Forced-choice question`: two meaningful alternatives followed by a decision rule.
13. `In medias res`: enter at the decisive action, then supply only necessary context.
14. `Open confession/lesson`: a real error, changed belief, or learned constraint with evidence.

### 4.6 Information Gaps Without Clickbait `[Research-backed principle + risk control]`
A valid curiosity gap:

- Names the subject.
- Gives enough information for the viewer to value the missing piece.
- Makes the scope of the answer finite.
- Begins paying off quickly.
- Closes the promised loop.

Reject:

- “You won’t believe…” with no subject.
- A shocking first frame unrelated to the message.
- Delaying a simple answer solely to inflate watch time.
- False binary choices.
- Withholding material conditions, price, sponsorship, or risk.
- A title/thumbnail promise that the opening reframes into something weaker.

### 4.7 First-Frame Tests `[Research-backed gist + platform-backed safe-zone/sound behavior + internal QA]`
Run all:

- `Gist test`: after a glance, can a reviewer identify the topic, subject/product, and category of value?
- `Sound-off test`: is the core premise still understandable?
- `Small-screen test`: are subject, text, and evidence legible on a phone?
- `Expectation test`: does the opening match the title, thumbnail, caption, and distribution context?
- `Crop/safe-zone test`: do UI overlays or alternate placements hide any essential element?
- `Credibility test`: is the claim plausible before explanation?

### 4.8 Hook Rewrite Procedure `[Internal operating method]`
When the opening underperforms:

1. Locate the failing stage: exposure, chose-to-view/start, first retention interval, or downstream action.
2. Inspect packaging–opening congruence before rewriting the words.
3. Name one failed review dimension: relevance, clarity, specificity, credibility, or continuity.
4. Preserve the body, audience, offer, placement, and distribution conditions where possible.
5. Rewrite one primary element: first visual, spoken line, text line, or proof preview.
6. Record the new hypothesis and whether the comparison is randomized or merely sequential.
7. Reject the rewrite if it wins early attention but damages trust, product connection, or downstream action.

Calibrated example:

- `Bad`: “You won’t believe what happens next.” It hides the subject, proof, and value.
- `Better for problem-aware players`: “Почему маг останавливается перед ударом — и почему это спасает позиционную игру.” It names the behavior and payoff.
- `Proof-led continuation`: show the same encounter with “move while attacking” versus “plant and attack,” then explain the control rule. If that proof cannot be shown, weaken the claim.

## 5. Retention: Delivering the Contract

### 5.1 Retention Is Not Constant Stimulation `[Research-backed coherence + internal synthesis]`
Retention comes from useful progression. Every beat must do at least one:

- Add new information.
- Demonstrate evidence.
- Change the stakes.
- Resolve part of the question.
- Create a necessary next question.
- Advance a cause-and-effect chain.
- Produce emotion that supports the message.

Random zooms, captions, cuts, sound effects, or movement do not repair a weak value sequence.

### 5.2 Generic Retention Spine `[Internal heuristic]`
For short social video when no more specific model below applies:

1. `Promise`: establish relevance and value.
2. `Orientation`: supply only the context required to understand the proof.
3. `Progressive payoff`: deliver useful intermediate answers, not one delayed reveal.
4. `Proof`: show mechanism, result, example, source, or credible experience.
5. `Resolution`: close the main loop.
6. `Action`: offer the next step that follows naturally from the resolution.

The first seconds are disproportionately important across platform guidance, but no universal edit cadence or ideal duration exists. Use the shortest duration that fully delivers the promise, then validate with account-specific retention.

### 5.3 Format-Specific Retention Models `[Internal heuristics; validate per account]`

| Format/job | Progression |
|---|---|
| Explanation/tutorial | Recognizable problem → outcome map → ordered steps → worked example/proof → decision summary |
| Product/mechanic demo | Result under named conditions → test criterion → process/action → contrast/control → result and limitation |
| Narrative/case | Disrupting event → goal/stakes → attempt → complication/choice → outcome → meaning/evidence |
| List/ranking | Decision criterion → item progression with distinct value → strongest evidence → synthesis, not a repeated list |
| Carousel | Standalone promise card → stakes/map → one advancing idea per card → proof/example → summary/CTA |
| Live | Immediate agenda/value → recurring reorientation for new arrivals → bounded segments → Q&A/participation → recap/action |

Narrative transportation can support attention and persuasion, but stories can also make a single case feel more representative or causal than it is. Pair consequential narrative claims with data, conditions, or counterexamples.

### 5.4 Beat Sheet `[Internal operating artifact]`
For every beat, record:

| Field | Required question |
|---|---|
| Time/range | Where does the beat occur? |
| Viewer question | What does the viewer need answered now? |
| Visual | What is visible, and what is the focal point? |
| Audio/voice | What new meaning does it add? |
| On-screen text | What must remain clear without sound? |
| Function | Promise, orientation, payoff, proof, resolution, or action? |
| Open loop | What remains unanswered, and is it legitimate? |
| Proof | What makes the claim credible? |
| Exit risk | Why might the viewer leave here? |

### 5.5 Retention Failure Taxonomy `[Platform-backed diagnostics + research-backed cognitive load + internal taxonomy]`
Diagnose the cause before changing the edit:

- `Expectation mismatch`: packaging promised a different asset.
- `Low relevance`: right topic, wrong audience situation.
- `Slow orientation`: preamble, greeting, logo animation, repeated setup.
- `Redundancy`: the asset repeats rather than advances.
- `Delayed value`: useful information is held hostage until the end.
- `Cognitive overload`: too many claims, entities, cuts, or simultaneous text/audio ideas.
- `Low legibility`: text, captions, subject, or proof cannot be perceived.
- `Weak causality`: beats are individually interesting but do not form a chain.
- `Trust break`: exaggerated claim, hidden condition, implausible result, or irrelevant stock media.
- `Product disconnect`: entertainment holds attention while the product/category disappears.
- `Premature action`: CTA arrives before value or proof.
- `Premature closure`: the question is fully answered before the asset ends.

### 5.6 Retention Metrics and Conditional Loss `[Platform metric definitions + internal analysis method]`
Store the platform definition and collection date beside every metric:

- `Chosen-to-view rate = views chosen / feed presentations`, only where the platform exposes both.
- `Stage retention R(t₂|t₁) = viewers remaining at t₂ / viewers remaining at t₁`.
- `Completion rate = completed plays / eligible starts`, using the platform’s own completion/start definitions.
- `Average percentage viewed = average view duration / asset duration`, noting loop/replay treatment.
- `Action rate = qualified actions / chosen denominator`; state whether denominator is reach, starts, viewers, clicks, or sessions.

Use conditional stage retention to find where loss concentrates. Never combine platform definitions into one cross-platform benchmark.

### 5.7 Reading Retention Data `[Platform-backed interpretation + causal caution]`
- Flat sections indicate sustained viewing, not necessarily comprehension or persuasion.
- Dips indicate skipping or exits; inspect the beat and expectation at that point.
- Spikes can mean delight, sharing, deliberate replay, or confusion. Validate with comments, playback context, and the beat itself.
- Gradual decline is normal; compare against similar formats, durations, audiences, and traffic sources.
- Segment new/returning, organic/paid, follower/non-follower, placement, and audience cohort where the platform allows it.
- Move late top moments earlier only if the causal context still works.
- Never diagnose a script from the aggregate completion rate alone.

The curve identifies `where`, not `why`. Treat the cause as a hypothesis until supported by a controlled comparison, repeated pattern, playback observation, or audience evidence. A sequential repost is confounded by audience mix, competition, time, and seasonality.

## 6. Media-Material Creation Rules

### 6.1 Brief Before Production `[Internal operating standard]`
Every asset brief must state:

- Business outcome and content job.
- Priority audience and evidence.
- One primary promise.
- One primary proof mechanism.
- One intended action.
- Platform, surface, placement, locale, and distribution mode.
- Master format and required placement-specific exports.
- Rights owner for every visual, audio, font, quote, testimonial, and likeness.
- Accessibility, disclosure, policy, and legal review requirements.
- Measurement event and decision rule.

### 6.2 Master Idea, Native Exports `[Platform-backed paid/organic + internal production standard]`
Do not upload one undifferentiated export everywhere. Preserve the idea, then adapt:

- Aspect ratio and composition.
- Safe zones and UI obstruction.
- Opening behavior and expected sound state.
- Caption density and line length.
- Thumbnail or cover.
- Caption/copy limits and visible first line.
- CTA mechanism.
- Platform culture and user intent.
- Current technical specifications.

Treat current official specifications as mutable production inputs, not timeless knowledge.

### 6.3 Export Matrix `[Mandatory risk control]`
Create one row per export; never rely on memory:

| Field | Required value |
|---|---|
| Platform/surface | Exact destination, such as Reels organic or Reels ad |
| Context | Organic, paid, partnership, creator post, live, or cross-post |
| Official spec source | Direct URL |
| Checked | Date and owner |
| Canvas | Aspect ratio and pixel dimensions |
| File | Container, codec, max size, frame rate, duration |
| Safe zone | Linked overlay/template version and date |
| Cover/thumbnail | Separate dimensions and crop previews |
| Captions | Native track, burned-in, both, or not applicable |
| Audio | Licensed source, intelligibility check, expected sound state |
| CTA/link | Native action, URL, UTM, landing match |
| Disclosure | Placement, wording, visual/audio treatment |
| Accessibility | Contrast, flash, text duration, alt/description |
| Rights/expiry | Asset ledger IDs and last permitted publication date |

If an official spec cannot be verified, mark the row `Blocked` rather than inventing dimensions.

### 6.4 Visual Hierarchy `[W3C-derived internal quality floor + platform-backed safe zones]`
- One dominant focal point per frame/card.
- Show the subject, product, result, or evidence early when it is central to the promise.
- Keep essential text, logos, faces, UI, product details, and disclosures inside the placement safe zone.
- Design and review at actual phone size.
- Use concise overlays that reinforce rather than duplicate long narration.
- As an internal quality floor, ensure text contrast of at least `4.5:1`; text around `24 px` regular or `18.5 px` bold at 1× web-equivalent scale may use `3:1`. These WCAG values are adopted for readability, not asserted as universal social-platform law.
- Do not encode essential meaning only through color, audio, or fleeting motion.
- Avoid background detail that competes with captions or proof.

### 6.5 Video `[Platform-backed openings + research-backed coherence + internal QA]`
- The opening frame must communicate; do not spend it on a generic logo slate or greeting.
- The hook, message, proof, and CTA must be one causal story.
- Produce for the expected platform experience, but preserve meaning both sound-on and sound-off where feasible.
- Provide accurate, synchronized captions for speech and meaningful audio; edit auto-captions.
- Captions must not obstruct relevant visual information.
- Use cuts and movement to reveal information or progress, not as empty stimulation.
- Make product placement engaging and message-relevant; total engagement disconnected from the product is not a conversion strategy.
- Use licensed audio and record its source, license, territory, term, and restrictions.
- As an internal accessibility floor, avoid content flashing more than three times in any one-second period unless it is verified below WCAG flash thresholds.
- For essential on-screen text, use the review starting point `max(1.5 seconds, 0.5 seconds + word count / 3)` and validate at phone size with native-language reviewers. This is an internal pacing heuristic, not a universal reading-speed law.

### 6.6 Captions, Audio Description, and Text Alternatives `[W3C-derived internal quality floor]`
- Prefer a correct native caption track when supported because it is editable, selectable, and available to assistive technology.
- Use burned-in captions when guaranteed in-frame visibility is required; if both native and burned-in captions are used, preview that they do not overlap.
- Caption dialogue, speaker changes when unclear, and meaningful non-speech sound. Proofread names, product terms, numbers, negation, and timing.
- Do not cover faces, UI, product evidence, disclosures, or other relevant information.
- If essential meaning exists only visually and is not already narrated, provide audio description, integrate that meaning into the main narration, or supply an equivalent text alternative according to the destination’s capabilities.
- A talking-head video whose meaningful visuals are already described in speech does not need redundant audio description.

### 6.7 Static Images and Carousels `[Internal heuristic + platform accessibility capabilities]`
- The first card must work as a standalone hook.
- Each card has one primary idea and advances the sequence.
- Preserve orientation: show progress, numbering, or a clear swipe reason when needed.
- Put critical information in visible text, not only in the platform caption.
- End with resolution and a proportionate CTA.
- Provide meaningful alt text where the platform supports it; describe purpose and information, not every decorative detail.
- Avoid screenshots of dense text when native readable text or a designed summary is possible.

### 6.8 Copy and Metadata `[Platform-backed organic/paid + risk control]`
- The visible first line must establish relevance or useful context.
- Title, thumbnail/cover, opening frame, caption, and CTA must describe the same promise.
- Use keywords, hashtags, sounds, and topics because they improve discoverability or context, not as decorative stuffing.
- Use concrete verbs and nouns; remove unsupported superlatives.
- State material conditions near the claim.
- Localize meaning, examples, reading speed, captions, and cultural references—not just words.
- Never fabricate customer language, testimonials, scarcity, authority, results, or consensus.

### 6.9 Placement Baselines `[Context-labeled platform guidance]`
These are starting points, not substitutes for a live spec check. Advertising results must not be represented as organic evidence:

| Surface | Evidence context | Baseline |
|---|---|---|
| TikTok short video | Official paid guidance; separate official organic playbook | Vertical and native; connect hook to message. Paid hook/CTA lift claims do not predict organic reach. |
| Instagram/Facebook Reels | Official paid guidance | `9:16`, audio considered, key messages inside safe zones for ads. Treat as a production baseline only for organic until organic evidence exists. |
| YouTube/Shorts | Official organic guidance | Packaging must match the opening; inspect chose-to-view, duration/percentage viewed, satisfaction, topic interest, competition, and seasonality. |
| LinkedIn feed video | Official paid guidance | Mobile-readable square or vertical creative, captions, strong opening, objective-specific metrics. Validate separately for organic. |
| Pinterest | Official paid guidance | Mobile-first vertical `2:3` starting point, visible product/brand, concise message, specific CTA. Validate separately for organic. |
| X video | Official paid guidance | Mobile-readable; movement early, captions, clear branding; choose aspect ratio per placement. Validate separately for organic. |
| Snapchat | Official paid guidance | Full-screen native storytelling, hero message from the opening frame, early offer/message, sound considered. |

### 6.10 Rights, Consent, Disclosure, and Claims `[Regulator/platform-backed + risk control]`
- Keep an asset ledger: creator, owner, source URL/file, license, permission evidence, territory, term, allowed edits, attribution, expiry.
- “Found online,” “credited,” “short clip,” and “noncommercial account” are not licenses.
- Fair use is a case-specific legal doctrine, not a production shortcut; commercial use, creative source material, amount used, and market substitution matter.
- Disclose any material relationship clearly and conspicuously in the content itself.
- For video endorsements, place disclosure in the video; visual plus audio is safer for mixed sound behavior.
- For stories/images, superimpose a readable disclosure long enough to notice.
- For live streams, repeat disclosure because viewers enter at different times.
- Do not hide disclosure after “more,” in a hashtag pile, on a profile page, or behind vague abbreviations.
- Do not claim experience the speaker did not have or results the brand cannot substantiate.
- Obtain a written UGC/likeness release defining channels, paid/organic use, edits, territory, term, revocation process, and compensation. A repost tag is not a release.
- For minors, require guardian authorization and jurisdiction-specific privacy/advertising review; do not infer consent from the minor’s upload.
- Minimize personal data in briefs, raw footage, screenshots, testimonials, filenames, and analytics exports; record retention and deletion owners.
- Check music-library terms for account type, territory, platform, paid versus organic use, synchronization, derivatives, and cross-posting. In-app availability on one platform does not grant cross-platform or advertising rights.
- Disclose realistic, meaningfully altered or synthetic people, events, places, voices, or scenes whenever the destination requires it; preserve provenance and consent even when labeling is not technically required.
- Escalate regulated, health, financial, safety, minor-directed, contest, privacy, testimonial, and comparative claims for jurisdiction-specific review.

## 7. Publishing and Community Operations

### 7.1 Preflight `[Internal operating standard]`
Before scheduling or publishing, verify:

- Correct account, platform, placement, locale, date, and timezone.
- Approved promise, copy, CTA, link, UTM, landing-page match, and offer conditions.
- Export, aspect ratio, resolution, duration, safe zones, cover, thumbnail, and audio state.
- Captions, text contrast, alt text, language, pronunciation, and reading time.
- Rights ledger, consent, disclosure, claims evidence, policy review, and expiry.
- Measurement events and baseline.
- Moderation owner, response boundaries, escalation path, and response window.

### 7.2 Timing and Cadence `[Platform-backed organic where available + internal capacity rule]`
- There is no universal best posting time or frequency.
- Start from audience activity, content shelf life, production capacity, moderation availability, and comparable account history.
- A cadence is valid only if quality, response capability, and learning velocity remain sustainable.
- Separate evergreen, timely, launch, reactive, and community programming; they require different planning and review speeds.

### 7.3 Community Response Matrix `[Internal operating standard]`
Classify responses:

- Question or missing context.
- Objection or trust barrier.
- Useful story or audience evidence.
- Product/support issue.
- Praise or advocacy opportunity.
- Misinformation requiring correction.
- Harassment, spam, safety, or policy escalation.

| Class | Default action | Escalation |
|---|---|---|
| Question/missing context | Answer with verified information or link; log repeated questions | Product/content owner if answer changes promise |
| Objection/criticism | Acknowledge the specific issue; answer facts; do not demand agreement | Owner when policy, pricing, safety, or roadmap is involved |
| Product/support issue | Capture minimum necessary detail and move to the approved support channel | Support owner; never solicit sensitive data publicly |
| Misinformation | Correct once with source and scope; avoid amplifying fringe claims | Legal/safety/communications for material harm |
| Praise/advocacy/UGC | Thank; request explicit reuse permission before republishing | Rights owner for reuse |
| Spam/harassment | Apply published moderation policy consistently; preserve evidence when needed | Safety/platform reporting |
| Credible threat, privacy leak, security, legal or safety event | Do not improvise; capture, restrict exposure if authorized, and escalate immediately | Named crisis owner |

Do not optimize for comment volume by provoking avoidable conflict. Extract recurring language and objections into the audience evidence sheet and future content hypotheses. Response-time targets are set by risk class, staffing, and business hours; there is no universal SMM SLA.

### 7.4 Crisis Protocol `[Risk control]`
1. Capture the post/comment, URL, timestamps, reach, and current actions without spreading sensitive data.
2. Validate facts and severity; distinguish criticism, service incident, coordinated abuse, safety event, and legal/privacy exposure.
3. Assign one incident owner and one approved spokesperson.
4. Pause scheduled content only when authorized and contextually necessary.
5. Publish a holding statement only when it adds verified information and a next-update commitment.
6. Maintain an incident log of decisions, approvals, removals/hiding, platform reports, and updates.
7. Close with correction/remedy and a postmortem; do not silently erase legitimate criticism to improve sentiment metrics.

## 8. Measurement and Experimentation

### 8.1 Metric Ladder `[Platform definitions + internal outcome alignment]`
Use the narrowest metric that matches the objective:

1. Eligible exposure and reach.
2. Start/stop or early-view behavior where available.
3. Retention curve, average view duration/percentage, quartiles, completion.
4. Saves, shares, qualified comments, profile actions, search lift, and negative feedback.
5. Landing-page action, lead, trial, purchase, or other conversion.
6. Returning viewers, repeat engagement, community participation, retention, or advocacy.

Platform “views” and engagement metrics have different definitions. Never compare raw counts across platforms without normalization and definition review.

### 8.2 Experiment Types `[Research-backed experiment principles + platform capabilities]`

- `Concurrent randomized test`: variants are allocated at the same time by a platform or controlled delivery system. This can support causal conclusions within the tested population.
- `Matched holdout/lift test`: a defined group does not receive the treatment; use only when assignment and contamination are understood.
- `Sequential organic comparison`: variants publish at different times to changing audiences. Treat as a quasi-test and directional evidence, not an A/B test.
- `Pre/post observation`: weakest design; topic, competition, audience, seasonality, product changes, and distribution can explain the difference.

Never call sequential reposts “A/B tests.” Prefer native concurrent tools when available; YouTube’s thumbnail Test & Compare, for example, explicitly distinguishes concurrent tests from third-party sequential rotation.

### 8.3 Creative Experiment Card `[Mandatory operating artifact]`
Record before launch:

- Observation.
- One falsifiable hypothesis.
- Audience and placement.
- Control.
- Variant and the single primary variable changed.
- Primary metric tied to the failure stage.
- Guardrail metrics.
- Run conditions and minimum evidence rule.
- Decision rule: keep, reject, iterate, or inconclusive.
- Risks and confounders.

Useful isolated variables include first visual, promise wording, proof timing, speaker, duration, CTA, caption style, and cover. Do not change hook, body, offer, audience, and distribution simultaneously and call the result a hook test.

### 8.4 Experiment Protocol `[Research-backed principles + internal risk control]`
Before launch:

1. Record baseline, unit of analysis, assignment method, population, placement, primary metric, guardrails, and minimum detectable effect (`MDE`).
2. Estimate required sample from baseline variance/rate, MDE, significance level, and power. There is no honest universal sample minimum.
3. If power cannot be estimated, label the work `Exploratory`; do not declare a winner from a small difference.
4. Run concurrent variants when the platform allows; keep audience eligibility, offer, body, destination, and delivery settings constant.
5. Predeclare a full window that covers relevant day/time cycles. Do not stop early merely when a preferred variant leads.
6. Monitor sample-ratio mismatch, delivery imbalance, tracking loss, novelty, seasonality, outside campaigns, creator reposts, and product events.
7. Correct for multiple comparisons or limit the number of primary hypotheses.
8. Report effect size and uncertainty, not only raw totals or “winner.”
9. Use `Inconclusive` when evidence is insufficient; absence of significance is not proof of equality.

### 8.5 Hook Test Matrix `[Internal test design]`
For one body and one audience, produce:

- Direct relevance hook.
- Demonstration/proof hook.
- Bounded curiosity or contrast hook.

Keep the payoff and offer constant. Evaluate early retention first, then downstream action and trust guardrails.

### 8.6 Diagnostic Map `[Internal hypothesis map; not causal proof]`

| Observation | Likely investigation |
|---|---|
| Low exposure, strong retention/action | Topic demand, packaging, eligibility, competition, distribution, sample size. |
| Exposure, weak opening retention | Audience–promise fit, first-frame gist, packaging mismatch, legibility, slow start. |
| Strong opening, mid-video dip | Redundancy, missing progression, overload, weak causality, delayed proof. |
| Strong watch, weak action | CTA, offer, landing match, product connection, wrong content job. |
| Strong engagement, weak conversion | Entertainment/product disconnect, low intent audience, vanity interaction, attribution gap. |
| High rewatch spike | Delight or useful density; also test for confusion. |
| High comments, poor sentiment | Controversy is not automatically success; inspect trust, safety, and audience quality. |
| Strong one-off post, weak return | No franchise, audience mismatch, novelty without durable value, weak library. |

## 9. Quality Gates

### Evidence Gate `[Mandatory]`
- Every material recommendation is labeled Observed, Platform-backed organic/paid, Research-backed, Internal heuristic, Hypothesis, Risk control, or Preference.
- Paid evidence is not represented as organic performance evidence.
- Context, transfer limits, and source/observation date are recorded.

### Strategy Gate `[Mandatory]`
- One business outcome, audience, content job, promise, proof, and action are explicit.
- Product truth and forbidden claims are known.

### Hook Gate `[Mandatory]`
- No relevance, clarity, specificity, credibility, or continuity dimension is `Fail`; every accepted `Weak` is recorded as a test risk.
- First-frame, sound-off, small-screen, expectation, safe-zone, and credibility tests pass.

### Retention Gate `[Mandatory]`
- Every beat advances meaning.
- The asset pays off progressively and closes the main loop.
- No removable preamble, redundancy, or hostage-style delayed value remains.

### Media Gate `[Mandatory]`
- Placement-specific export reviewed at phone size.
- Export matrix, captions/alternative, contrast, flash, text duration, safe zones, cover, audio, and CTA pass.

### Trust and Compliance Gate `[Mandatory risk control]`
- Verifiable factual and product-performance claims are substantiated.
- Rights and consent are recorded.
- Every material connection—payment, gift, employment, personal/family relationship, discount, or other value—is conspicuous when applicable.
- Required AI/synthetic-media labeling and provenance checks pass.
- Required legal/policy reviews are complete.

### Measurement Gate `[Mandatory]`
- Metric definitions, hypothesis, experiment type, baseline/control, guardrails, assignment, run window, power status, and decision rule are recorded before publication.

## 10. Anti-Patterns
- “Use a hook” without defining audience, promise, proof, and continuation.
- Treating “pattern interrupt every N seconds” as a universal law.
- Starting with greetings, credentials, or a logo before relevance.
- Copying trends without category or audience fit.
- Measuring conversion content by likes or awareness content by last-click sales alone.
- Declaring a spike successful without checking whether it is confusion.
- Making one master export and cropping it blindly for every placement.
- Captioning only the spoken words while omitting meaningful sounds or speaker changes.
- Using “fair use” as an assumption.
- Hiding sponsorship in hashtags or metadata.
- Rewriting several variables after one underperforming post.
- Presenting platform guidance, benchmarks, or algorithms as timeless facts.
- Calling sequential organic posts an A/B test.
- Treating paid-playbook lift claims as evidence of organic ranking.
- Using an unexplained numeric quality score as if it predicted performance.
